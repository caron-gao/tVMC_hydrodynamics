\begin{abstract}
We show that, within the manifold where the Jastrow pair factor is
frozen to its ground-state form, time-dependent variational Monte Carlo
(tVMC) based on a Jastrow--Feenberg wave function maps exactly onto
irrotational superfluid hydrodynamics.  The theory is then closed by an
equation of state extracted from a single variational Monte Carlo
calculation of the uniform system; within the local-density
approximation, this yields a generalized Gross--Pitaevskii equation with
a density-dependent chemical potential $\mu(\rho)$.  For a trapped
microwave-shielded dipolar Bose gas, the reduced dynamics reproduces the
full tVMC breathing oscillations for $N=8$--$128$ with below-1\%
frequency error and $\sim\!5\%$ amplitude error, while lowering the
computational cost by more than an order of magnitude.  Our results
establish a first-principles route from correlated many-body wave
functions to predictive hydrodynamics for molecular gases, quantum
droplets, and supersolids.
\end{abstract}

%======================================================================
\emph{Introduction.}---Microwave-shielded polar molecules have emerged
as a leading platform for dipolar quantum matter.  Recent experiments
have achieved Bose--Einstein
condensation~\cite{Bigagli2024,Shi2025}, observed shielded
collisions~\cite{Anderegg2021,Schindewolf2022}, and probed few- and
many-body phenomena~\cite{Chen2024,Lin2023,Duda2023}.  With permanent
dipole moments orders of magnitude larger than those of magnetic
atoms~\cite{Chomaz2023,Lahaye2009}, shielded molecules are expected to
exhibit rich collective behavior---from quantum
droplets~\cite{Petrov2015,Wachtler2016} to supersolid-like
phases---in a widely tunable setting.  Collective oscillations and
expansion dynamics serve as primary experimental probes of the
underlying interactions in trapped
BECs~\cite{Dalfovo1999,Jin1996,Mewes1996}; for shielded molecular
condensates such measurements remain an open frontier, making
first-principles predictions especially timely.

For magnetic-atom dipolar BECs, the extended Gross--Pitaevskii equation
(eGPE)---incorporating the non-local dipolar mean field and a
perturbative Lee--Huang--Yang (LHY)
correction~\cite{LimaPelster2011}---has successfully described quantum
droplets and collective excitations~\cite{Wachtler2016,Chomaz2023}.
This framework, however, relies on a Born-approximation coupling
constant and treats quantum fluctuations perturbatively; indeed,
quantum Monte Carlo calculations have revealed significant deviations
from eGPE predictions even for magnetic
atoms~\cite{Bottcher2019}.  For microwave-shielded molecules the
situation is more challenging: the strong short-range repulsion
($\sim C_6/r^6$) produces a deep correlation hole that cannot be
captured perturbatively, and without it the bare interaction integral
diverges.  Time-dependent variational Monte Carlo
(tVMC)~\cite{Carleo2012,Carleo2014,Carleo2017PRX} can propagate a
correlated many-body wave function in real time, but its stochastic
sampling at every time step severely limits accessible system sizes and
evolution times.  What is missing is a controlled coarse-graining that
retains the microscopic content of tVMC while recovering the efficiency
of hydrodynamic evolution.

Here we establish a first-principles reduction from correlated tVMC to
macroscopic hydrodynamics.  The key physical ingredient is a separation
of scales: short-range pair correlations relax much faster than the
global density profile.  Under this condition, freezing the two-body
Jastrow factor $u_2$ makes the time-dependent variational principle map
exactly onto irrotational superfluid hydrodynamics.  We verify this
scenario from full tVMC, then close the theory with an equation of
state extracted from a single uniform-system VMC calculation within the
local-density approximation, yielding a generalized GPE.  The reduced
theory reproduces the full tVMC breathing dynamics for $N=8$ and
$N=128$, while extending directly to experimentally relevant large-$N$
regimes beyond direct tVMC.

%======================================================================
\emph{Model and variational dynamics.}---We consider $N$ bosons in a
cylindrically symmetric harmonic trap with Hamiltonian
\begin{equation}
\hat{H}(t) = \sum_{i=1}^N\!\left[
-\tfrac{1}{2}\nabla_i^2 + V_{\mathrm{tr}}(\br_i,t)\right]
+ \sum_{i<j} v(\br_{ij}),
\label{eq:H}
\end{equation}
where $V_{\mathrm{tr}} = \frac{1}{2}[\omega_\rho(t)^2\rho^2 +
\omega_z^2 z^2]$ (natural units $\hbar=m=1$) and the two-body
interaction models microwave-shielded dipolar molecules:
\begin{equation}
v(\br) = \frac{C_3}{r^3}(3\cos^2\!\theta-1)
+ \frac{C_6}{r^6}(\sin^4\!\theta + w_{12}\sin^2\!\theta\cos^2\!\theta).
\label{eq:V}
\end{equation}
This effective potential, derived from Floquet scattering theory for
dual-microwave-dressed molecules~\cite{Deng2025}, is parametrized by
coefficients $C_3$, $C_6$, and $w_{12}$ fixed by the microwave field
configuration.  For the representative case studied here we choose
$C_3=10^{-3}$, $C_6=10^{-6}$, and $w_{12}=2.0$ in oscillator units,
initialize the system in the isotropic trap $\omega_\rho=\omega_z=1$,
and excite radial breathing dynamics with a quench
$\omega_\rho:1\to 2$ at $t=0$.

The many-body wavefunction is a truncated
Jastrow--Feenberg~\cite{Jastrow1955,Feenberg1969} ansatz:
\begin{equation}
\Psi(\bx,t) = \exp\!\Big[\sum_i u_1(\br_i,t)
+ \sum_{i<j} u_2(r_{ij},\hat\br_{ij})\Big],
\label{eq:Psi}
\end{equation}
where the complex one-body field $u_1(\br,t)$ controls the evolving
density and phase, while $u_2$ encodes pair correlations.  In
cylindrical geometry we use a separable parametrization for $u_1$, and
expand $u_2$ in partial waves as
\begin{equation}
u_2(r,\cos\theta) = u_{\mathrm{cusp}}(r) + \sum_{\ell=0,2,4}
f_\ell(r)\,P_\ell(\cos\theta),
\label{eq:u2}
\end{equation}
where $u_{\mathrm{cusp}}(r)$ enforces the short-range scattering
boundary condition and each $f_\ell$ is represented by Gaussian basis
functions~\cite{SM_note}.  For the present parameters the $\ell=2$
anisotropy is weak and the $\ell=4$ channel is negligible, so the pair
correlation is close to isotropic.

Figure~\ref{fig:hnc}(a) characterizes the optimized pair correlations.
The lowest-order hypernetted-chain estimate
$g_2^{\mathrm{HNC}}(r,\theta)=e^{2u_2(r,\cos\theta)}$~\cite{Hansen2006}
shows a short-range correlation hole produced by the shielding
repulsion, with only weak angular dependence.

The TDVP~\cite{Dirac1930,McLachlan1964} projects the Schr\"odinger
equation onto the variational tangent space.  Writing
$O_\alpha=\partial_{\theta_\alpha}\ln\Psi$ and
$E_L=(\hat{H}\Psi)/\Psi$, the equations of motion are~\cite{Carleo2012}
\begin{equation}
\sum_\beta S_{\alpha\beta}\,\dot\theta_\beta = -i\,F_\alpha,
\label{eq:tdvp}
\end{equation}
where the overlap matrix and force vector are
\begin{align}
S_{\alpha\beta} &= \avg{\Delta O_\alpha^*\,\Delta O_\beta}_t,
\label{eq:S}\\
F_\alpha &= \avg{\Delta O_\alpha^*\,\Delta E_L}_t,
\label{eq:F}
\end{align}
with $\Delta A = A - \avg{A}_t$ denoting connected fluctuations.  Full
Monte Carlo and convergence details are given in Ref.~\cite{SM_note}.

%======================================================================
\emph{Frozen-$u_2$ reduction.}---The reduction is motivated by a clear
separation of scales: the pair correlation $u_2$ is governed by the
interaction potential $v(r)$ and adjusts on the fast time scale
$\tau_{\mathrm{pair}} \sim r_0^2$ set by the range $r_0$ of the
repulsive shield, whereas the trap quench drives density redistribution
on the much slower trap time scale $\sim 1/\omega$.  This
hierarchy---analogous to the Born--Oppenheimer separation between fast
electronic and slow nuclear degrees of
freedom~\cite{PitaevskiiStringari2016}---suggests that the
pair-correlation sector remains adiabatically pinned to its
ground-state form while only the one-body field $u_1$ evolves.  The
comparison between full tVMC and this frozen-$u_2$ approximation
confirms the picture quantitatively: Fig.~\ref{fig:hnc}(b) shows that
the frozen-$u_2$ evolution closely tracks the full tVMC dynamics for
$N=128$, validating the restriction to fixed $u_2$.

When the variational degree of freedom is the continuous field
$u_1(\br,t)$, the TDVP logarithmic derivative becomes the microscopic
density operator:
\begin{equation}
O(\br;\bx) = \frac{\delta\ln\Psi}{\delta u_1(\br)}
= \sum_i\delta^{(3)}(\br-\br_i) = \hat\rho(\br).
\label{eq:O_rho}
\end{equation}
Because $u_2$ is real and frozen, the many-body phase is purely
one-body and therefore irrotational, $\bv = \nabla(\mathrm{Im}\, u_1)$;
no backflow terms arise.  The resulting potential flow precludes vortex
formation, so the approximation is tailored to collective modes and
quench dynamics that remain irrotational.

Decomposing $u_1 = a + i\theta$ (real amplitude and phase), the TDVP
action reduces to the standard hydrodynamic form~\cite{SM_note}.  The
reduction is exact within this frozen-$u_2$ manifold and yields the
continuity and Euler equations:
\begin{align}
&\partial_t\rho + \nabla\!\cdot(\rho\bv) = 0,
\label{eq:cont}\\
&\partial_t\bv + \nabla\!\Big(\tfrac{1}{2}v^2 + V_{\mathrm{tr}}
+ \mu_{u_2}[\rho]\Big) = 0,
\label{eq:euler}
\end{align}
where $\mu_{u_2}[\rho] = \delta E_{\mathrm{int}}[\rho;u_2]/\delta\rho$
is the generally nonlocal chemical-potential functional determined by
the frozen correlations.

To obtain a closed local evolution equation, we next invoke the
local-density approximation (LDA).  The internal energy then takes the
form $E_{\mathrm{int}} = \int\!\rho\,\varepsilon(\rho)\,d^3r$, where
$\varepsilon(\rho)$ is the energy per particle of the \emph{uniform}
system at density $\rho$ with the same frozen $u_2$.  This
approximation is valid when the density varies slowly on the scale of
the pair-correlation range, $|\nabla\rho|\,r_0/\rho \ll 1$, a
condition well satisfied in the bulk of the trapped cloud.  The
chemical potential becomes a local function,
\begin{equation}
\mu(\rho) = \frac{d[\rho\varepsilon(\rho)]}{d\rho}
= \varepsilon + \rho\varepsilon'.
\label{eq:mu}
\end{equation}
The hydrodynamic theory therefore closes in GP form, except that the
usual contact term $g\rho$ is replaced by the VMC-derived function
$\mu(\rho)$.

The near-stationarity of the pair correlations during the quench is
visualized directly in Fig.~\ref{fig:u2_heatmap}, which shows the
change in the lowest-order HNC pair correlation $\Delta e^{2u_2}$ at
four time snapshots.  The relative variation remains below $\sim 3\%$
throughout the breathing cycle, even as the one-body density undergoes
order-unity redistribution, confirming that $u_2$ is effectively
frozen on the dynamical time scale.

%======================================================================
\emph{Equation of state and generalized GPE.}---We extract
$\varepsilon(\rho)$ from a one-time VMC calculation on the
uniform system (periodic boundary conditions) with $u_2$ frozen at its
trapped ground-state values (computational details are given in~\cite{SM_note}).
Over the relevant density range, we fit the results to a polynomial
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon(\rho) = a_1\rho + a_2\rho^2 + \cdots,
\label{eq:eos}
\end{equation}
yielding the chemical potential
$\mu(\rho) = 2a_1\rho + 3a_2\rho^2+\cdots$.

The striking finding is that $\mu(\rho)$ is nearly linear over the
density range relevant to the trapped dynamics
(Fig.~\ref{fig:eos}), so the EOS is dominated by the leading term
$\mu \approx 2a_1\rho$.  This has the same functional form as the
standard GPE nonlinearity $g|\psi|^2$, with a \emph{renormalized
coupling} $g_{\mathrm{eff}} = 2a_1$.  Physically, the frozen pair
correlations---including the short-range correlation hole visible in
Fig.~\ref{fig:hnc}(a)---regularize the bare interaction integral
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_{\mathrm{int}}(\rho) = \frac{\rho}{2}
\int g_2(\br;\rho)\,v(\br)\,d^3r,
\label{eq:eps_int}
\end{equation}
which would diverge for the bare $C_3/r^3$ potential with
$g_2=1$.  The non-trivial pair structure encoded in $u_2$ renders this
integral finite and maps the realistic anisotropic interaction onto a
single effective coupling $g_{\mathrm{eff}}$.

This construction differs from phenomenological GP theories in two
respects.  First, $g_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is fixed by the correlated
many-body wave function rather than by a scattering-length
approximation; for the present parameters we obtain
$a_1 \approx 0.134$ from $N=32$ particles in the periodic simulation
cell, converged to within $3\%$ of the $N\to\infty$ extrapolation
$a_1^\infty \approx 0.137$~\cite{SM_note}.  Second, the small quadratic
correction $a_2\rho^2$ captures the residual density dependence of the
pair correlations---an effective three-body contribution that
emerges naturally from the VMC-derived EOS rather than being inserted
ad hoc.

Unlike the eGPE~\cite{LimaPelster2011,Wachtler2016}, which treats the
dipolar interaction through a non-local mean-field kernel supplemented
by a perturbative LHY correction, the present approach folds the full
interaction---including all non-perturbative pair correlations---into a
local function $\mu(\rho)$, extracted once from the uniform-system VMC.
This is both computationally simpler (no long-range convolutions at
each time step) and microscopically more faithful.  The near-linearity
of $\mu(\rho)$ is characteristic of the weak-interaction regime
($C_3 = 10^{-3}$); for stronger dipolar coupling, the higher-order
polynomial terms become essential, and the framework accommodates this
naturally through the full $\mu(\rho)$ nonlinearity in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:gpe_mu}.

The resulting generalized GPE,
\begin{equation}
i\partial_t\psi = \Big[-\tfrac{1}{2}\nabla^2
+ V_{\mathrm{tr}} + \mu(|\psi|^2)\Big]\psi,
\label{eq:gpe_mu}
\end{equation}
thus takes the standard GP form with the contact coupling replaced by
the VMC-derived $\mu(\rho)$; the near-linearity of the EOS explains
why a mean-field-like equation captures the correlated dynamics so
accurately.

%======================================================================
\emph{GPE solver.}---We solve Eq.~\eqref{eq:gpe_mu}
where $\psi = \sqrt\rho\,e^{i\theta}$ and the Laplacian provides both
quantum pressure and flow kinetic energy.  The equation is discretized
on a cylindrical $(r,z)$ grid exploiting azimuthal symmetry, evaluated
with a fourth-order finite-difference Laplacian and time-integrated
using a Runge--Kutta scheme under a dispersive CFL condition~\cite{SM_note}.

The initial state is obtained by imaginary-time propagation to
self-consistency: $\psi \to \psi - d\tau\,\hat H\psi$, normalized to
$N$ particles at each step, until the chemical potential converges to
$\delta\mu < 10^{-8}$.

%======================================================================
\emph{Validation against tVMC.}---We benchmark the generalized GPE
against frozen-$u_2$ tVMC at both weak and moderate interaction
strength.  For $N=8$, the GPE ground state reproduces the tVMC moments
to $\sim\!1\%$ and the energy to 0.7\% (Table~S1~\cite{SM_note}).  The
breathing frequency is $\omega_{\mathrm{br}} = 4.19 \approx 2\omega_\rho$
in both methods, and the oscillation amplitudes of $\avg{\rho^2}$ and
$\avg{z^2}$ agree to within $\sim\!1\%$.

For $N=128$, where the interaction energy reaches $\sim 21\%$ of the
total, the same EOS-based GPE remains accurate.  The ground-state
moments agree with tVMC to $3.5\%$ for $\avg{\rho^2}$ and $4.2\%$ for
$\avg{z^2}$, while the total energy differs by only $0.4\%$.
The few-percent offset in moments is consistent with a residual LDA
error at higher density.  The dynamical benchmark in
Fig.~\ref{fig:dynamics}(a) is likewise preserved: both approaches give
$\omega_{\mathrm{br}} = 4.19$, and the $\avg{\rho^2}(t)$ amplitude agrees
to about 5\%.  Notably, although the quench acts only on $\omega_\rho$,
the axial moment $\avg{z^2}(t)$ also oscillates---driven by nonlinear
radial--axial coupling through the interaction energy---and the GPE
captures this cross-coupling quantitatively
[Fig.~\ref{fig:dynamics}(a)].

The computational gain is substantial.  For $N=128$, the GPE evolution,
including imaginary-time state preparation, takes $\sim 70$ minutes on
a single CPU core, compared with $\sim 6$ hours on a modern GPU for the
full tVMC.

\emph{Large-$N$ prediction.}---Having benchmarked the reduction, we use
it in a regime inaccessible to direct tVMC.  With the same interaction
($C_3 = 10^{-3}$), the generalized GPE predicts sustained breathing
oscillations for $N=8192$ over six trap periods
[Fig.~\ref{fig:dynamics}(b)], with excellent energy conservation
($|\delta E/E| < 2.5\times 10^{-4}$).

The $N=8192$ system is qualitatively more strongly interacting than the
benchmarked cases: the interaction energy comprises $\sim 38\%$ of the
total (compared with $3.5\%$ for $N=8$ and $21\%$ for $N=128$), and
the peak density reaches $\rho_0 \approx 43$.  Despite this tenfold
variation in interaction fraction, the breathing frequency remains
$\omega_{\mathrm{br}} \approx 4.19$ from $N=8$ to $N=8192$.  This
robustness is a direct consequence of the near-linearity of
$\mu(\rho)$: for a strictly linear chemical potential, the scaling
ansatz~\cite{CastinDum1996,Stringari1996} predicts a breathing
frequency that depends only on the trap geometry and is independent of
the coupling strength.  The quench also drives pronounced axial
oscillations: even though $\omega_z$ is unchanged, $\avg{z^2}$
oscillates with a relative amplitude of $\sim 130\%$
[Fig.~\ref{fig:dynamics}(b)], reflecting strong radial--axial coupling
mediated by the interaction at large $N$.

This particle number falls within the range achieved in recent
molecular BEC experiments ($N \sim 10^3$--$10^4$)~\cite{Bigagli2024,Shi2025}.
Consequently, the predicted breathing dynamics is directly measurable via
experiments, providing a
stringent test of the VMC-derived equation of state.

%======================================================================
\emph{Discussion.}---The success of the frozen-$u_2$ hydrodynamic
reduction rests on two pillars: (i)~a separation of time scales---pair
correlations equilibrate much faster than the macroscopic density
redistribution, keeping $u_2$ nearly stationary---and (ii)~the fact
that integrating out these correlations yields an EOS dominated by a
single renormalized coupling $g_{\mathrm{eff}}$.

The near-linearity of $\mu(\rho)$ explains, at a deeper level, why a
GPE-like equation captures the correlated dynamics so faithfully even
though the microscopic pair correlation $g_2(r)\!=\!e^{2u_2}$ differs
markedly from the mean-field value $g_2\!=\!1$.  For the
hydrodynamic evolution only the functional form of $\mu(\rho)$ matters,
not the microscopic origin of its coefficients: once the pair
correlations have been integrated into $g_{\mathrm{eff}}$, the
dynamics is insensitive to the detailed shape of $g_2$.  The value of
$g_{\mathrm{eff}}$, however, \emph{cannot} be obtained from a
mean-field ($g_2\!=\!1$) treatment, because the bare dipolar integral
diverges; the non-trivial correlation hole produced by the shielding
repulsion is essential to regularize this integral and fix the
coupling.  The VMC-derived EOS therefore provides a first-principles
route to the effective interaction strength that phenomenological GP
theories must take as an external input.

The main quantitative limitation visible here is the few-percent offset
in ground-state moments at $N=128$, which points to gradient corrections
beyond the LDA~\cite{Ancilotto2017}; such terms, of the form
$\propto(\nabla\rho)^2/\rho$, could be systematically extracted from
VMC at spatially modulated densities and incorporated as a beyond-LDA
energy functional.  The reduction is expected to break down for
quenches faster than the pair-equilibration time
($\tau_{\mathrm{quench}} \lesssim \tau_{\mathrm{pair}}$), for
protocols that nucleate vortices, or near phase transitions (e.g., to a
quantum droplet or supersolid) where the pair correlations acquire
strong density dependence.  Within its domain of validity, the
framework extends naturally to other collective modes (scissors,
quadrupole), time-dependent trap geometries, and two-component
molecular mixtures relevant to current
experiments~\cite{Bigagli2024,Lin2023,Duda2023}.

%======================================================================
\emph{Conclusion.}---We have demonstrated that correlated many-body
dynamics from tVMC maps exactly onto superfluid hydrodynamics within the
frozen-$u_2$ manifold, with the required equation of state obtained from
a single uniform-system VMC calculation.  The resulting generalized GPE
reproduces the breathing dynamics of microwave-shielded dipolar bosons
from $N=8$ to $N=128$ with below-1\% frequency error and $\sim\!5\%$
amplitude error, and predicts a robust breathing frequency
$\omega_{\mathrm{br}} \approx 4.19$ that persists from $N=8$ to
$N=8192$ as the interaction fraction grows from $3.5\%$ to $38\%$---a
prediction directly testable in current molecular BEC experiments.
More broadly, the reduction establishes a general strategy for linking
correlated variational wave functions to predictive macroscopic
dynamics: extract an equation of state from VMC on the uniform system,
then evolve via hydrodynamics.  This route should be applicable to
other quantum fluids with strong short-range correlations, including
quantum droplets~\cite{Petrov2015,Bottcher2019} and dipolar
supersolids~\cite{Chomaz2023}, wherever the pair-correlation sector
equilibrates faster than the macroscopic dynamics of interest.
